How did I estimate what may happen to pending EB1A and NIW cases?

The first step was to measure pending-case maturation in the broader parent categories EB1 and EB2.

For each fiscal year with both observations available, I compared:

1. "actual": the last same-year USCIS snapshot, normally Q4;
2. "historic": the latest later snapshot for the same fiscal-year cohort.

The paired sample covers FY2019-FY2025. I removed decisions already present in the actual snapshot:

new approvals from pending = historic approvals - actual approvals
new denials from pending = historic denials - actual denials

This isolates the aggregate outcome mix added after the early snapshot.

Across the paired years:

• EB1 denial share among early actual decisions: 13.5%
• EB1 denial share among later-resolved pending cases: 27.5%
• Pending EB1 denial odds were 2.44x the early-decision odds
• Bootstrap interval for the multiplier: 1.66x-3.67x

• EB2 denial share among early actual decisions: 4.7%
• EB2 denial share among later-resolved pending cases: 27.6%
• Pending EB2 denial odds were 7.67x the early-decision odds
• Bootstrap interval: 4.27x-18.96x

A paired Wilcoxon test produced p = 0.0156 for both categories. This is meaningful exploratory evidence, but the sample contains only seven paired fiscal years.

I also checked accounting consistency. Increases in approvals plus denials were compared with decreases in pending inventory. All transitions were non-negative, and the maximum reconciliation gap was 5.13% of actual pending, consistent with USCIS revisions between releases.

The conclusion from this stage is important: early decided cases are not a random sample of the final population. The unresolved tail historically produced a substantially less favorable outcome mix.
