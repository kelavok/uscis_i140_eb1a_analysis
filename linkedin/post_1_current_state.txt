EB1A and NIW denial rates have changed dramatically. Here is the short version.

I analyzed USCIS I-140 reports from FY2009-FY2026 Q1 and modeled how today's pending inventory may eventually resolve.

E11 / EB1A

• Among cases already decided in FY2026 Q1, 52.5% were denied.
• In FY2022 Q1, that figure was 26.1%.
• After accounting for pending cases, the modeled final denial share is approximately 60.2%, with a scenario range of 45.8%-72.5%.
• That is an increase of 34.1 percentage points compared with FY2022 Q1.
• Q4 pending inventory grew from 6,281 in FY2022 to 21,157 in FY2025: a 3.4x increase.

NIW

• Among cases already decided in FY2026 Q1, 57.4% were denied.
• In FY2022 Q1, the denial share was only 6.6%.
• After accounting for pending cases, the modeled final denial share is approximately 72.8%, with a scenario range of 53.0%-85.8%.
• That is an increase of 66.2 percentage points compared with FY2022 Q1.
• Q4 pending inventory grew from 18,599 in FY2022 to 74,392 in FY2025: almost exactly 4x.

How was this estimated?

I compared early annual USCIS snapshots with later historical snapshots to measure how old pending cases actually turned into approvals and denials. I then adjusted those parent-category results using quarterly differences between E11 and EB1, and between NIW and EB2.

The main takeaway is not that every case now has the same probability of denial. USCIS aggregate data cannot track individual petitions, RFEs, premium processing, or case quality. But the direction is difficult to ignore: denial pressure increased sharply while pending inventory multiplied.
